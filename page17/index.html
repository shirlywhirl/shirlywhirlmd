A post shared by Shirlene Obuobi, M.D. (@shirlywhirlmd) on Friday, February 5, 2021

A black woman inspired me to go into Cardiology. Only in her 40s, she had terrible heart failure that had gone undiagnosed for years, and had been admitted to the hospital several times in the last month. Over the course of her admission, I got to know her some. She told me about the early symptoms that she, and later, the doctors she first presented to, ignored. About the impossibilities of making her appointments with her inflexible service job.

Since that patient , I’ve met many more patients like her. That’s why I’ve chosen to #RockARedLipChallenge and #GoRed- to raise awareness that Black women are at an exceptional risk for Cardiovascular Diseases (CVD) as presented by the American Heart Association.

❣️I #GoRed underneath my mask to signify the silent threat of heart disease. ❣️I #GoRed because we can’t just eliminate health disparities without the first step: education. ❣️I #GoRed because from now through May, @goclove will be dedicating their dollars and platform to support the @american_heart’s efforts to break down health inequities. ❣️ I # nominate @edna.md , @daniellebelardomd , @drmarthagulati, @ssg.to.mdphd, and @physiciandoodles to #GoRed: to be more aggressive in our efforts to detect CVD facing Black womxn both inside and outside the hospital.

Late post!

I haven’t seen my parents in a long time (over a year for my dad, 8+ months for my mom.) Our family is close in spirit but spread out across the country, and because of residency, I’ve missed all the major holidays. And because I’ve been treating COVID patients since March, I’ve avoided going home to see them. It’s been hard. In many ways, seeing my family and my best friends (Linda and I made a point to see each other three times a year in our first two years of residency) has been how I fill my tank. And without those moments to look forward to… Well, let’s just say that this year has been hard for everyone. Most of all, for the families of the 433,000 people who have died from COVID-19.

So to be able to go home and actually hug my parents without fearing for their lives? It’s awesome. I’m so grateful for this vaccine and for this moment. Things aren’t quite back to normal yet, and might not be for a while…. but I’m glad to get this little piece of normalcy back.

One question I get asked somewhat regularly is how I feel comfortable being so transparent about my beliefs/ myself. And I think the real answer is - I honestly really love me a lot. The way I see it, I’ve already given a lot to medicine, and so I’m not about to give up what I stand for or sanitize who I am. I realized fairly recently that trying to change myself to placate others didn’t make me happy, and so I… stopped (for better or worse lol.) I know my value, and know what I can contribute.

Not that this isn’t still an ongoing process. My self esteem used to be in the dirt, and one thing that took me a while to do is accept compliments without qualifying them. I occasionally have to beat back my imposter syndrome, and I have a few bad habits to break. The other day, for example , my boss Advanced HF attending told me to stop prefacing my questions with “sorry if this is a dumb question” bc it diminishes myself, and I took that advice to heart bc it IS.

Anyway I’m more humble than this irl but this is indeed what is going on in my head 😎

Recently, I did two weeks of Palliative Care consults. During that rotation, I had the opportunity to actually give patients/ their families time to discuss their symptoms/ concerns and answer their questions.

Some context- my team normally covers 10-20 patients (of varying acuity). We get roughly one day off a week, and between rounding, patient care, documentation, etc, most of our days are 10 hours long. Patients/families put an enormous amount of trust in their medical teams; asking questions about labs/ medications/ plans outside of rounds is actually totally reasonable. But honestly? If we gave every patient 30 minutes a day to ask additional questions, we would be stuck in the hospital for 3-6 more hours. Thus, throughout residency, I’ve treated a lot of those pages for additional info with frustration/ trepidation. (Some of that is bc a lot of those conversations also become an opportunity for family/ patients to transfer their frustration onto us but that’s a convo for another day.)

Still- it was really gratifying to actually be able to hold space for patients/ families in the way they deserve.

You know, I thought really hard about doing a comic about recent events, but… I’m tired of devoting energy to that and instead want to highlight the foot soldiers of our profession - primary care doctors!

Only one member of my residency class is pursuing primary care (and she is probably one of the best doctors I know as is/ I would put my parents on her panel lol.) This doesn’t come as a huge surprise to me - being a primary care doctor has been one of the hardest aspects of residency. I’m only just starting to enjoy it (Bc I love my patients), but I won’t lie and say that I’m excited to never do it again…

Our PCPs are overworked, underpaid, and under appreciated. Our insurance system relies on them to fill in impossible gaps in care, and that, on top of the bulk of the documentation, they also have to piecemeal together the decisions of numerous specialists often without access to info.

I could say more but I’m tired. Regardless, tell your PCP you appreciate them!!!!
